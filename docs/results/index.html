A Skeptic's Guide to Marketing Mix Modeling

Results & recommendations

What 11 graded datasets say about trusting MMM — and what to do about it.

The bottom line

We ran a controlled competition: a synthetic world where every channel's true effect is sealed in an answer key, and a dozen MMM approaches — naive regression, our Bayesian model, Google Meridian, Meta Robyn (the real R package), a spend-ladder experiment — all graded against that truth across many independent datasets. Three results matter more than any single number:

  1. There is no single "best" MMM. Across 11 datasets the top engines (Meridian, our PyMC, Robyn-style) are tied within the run-to-run noise; the apparent winner changes with the data. Anyone who shows you one leaderboard and declares a champion is fooling themselves. But the choice is not arbitrary either — different engines suit different data (see the which-engine-when guide): the spend ladder earns its keep when channels are saturated, while a flexible engine like Robyn does relatively better with headroom.
  2. An observational MMM is only as trustworthy as its confound control. Spend chases demand (here, correlation ~0.6 with season), so without strong seasonality controls — and ideally a randomized experiment — attribution is biased in a direction you cannot know from the fit.
  3. Goodness-of-fit lies. Engines hit R² 0.85–0.99 while mis-crediting channels by ±50–120%. Fit quality tells you almost nothing about decomposition quality.

What we learned, by approach

  • Regularisation is not optional. The two engines that diverged on real datasets — frequentist NLS and the raw spend-ladder curve fit — were the two with unregularised least squares. Adding a prior/ridge fixed both (NLS: a seed hit MAE 47,000 → ~130; ladder: max 360 → 98). In thin-data, many-parameter MMM, priors are what keep the fit on the road.
  • Control seasonality, idiomatically. Meridian's AKS knots and a Fourier basis are two roads to the same end; without either, the spend↔season confound roughly doubles attribution.
  • Geo data buys precision, not immunity. A geo-level model is sharper, but against an unobserved geo confounder it becomes confidently wrong (+120% bias, 0/6 intervals covering truth). Even a near-perfect demand-proxy control only claws back about half. More observational data is not a substitute for randomization.
  • Experiments are the anchor — but feed them correctly. Bolting a single lift number onto a model as a scalar prior usually hurt (over-credited by 100%+). Curve-aware calibration (feed the lift at its measured exposure, or trace the curve with a spend ladder) is the right way — and it is still capped by the experiment's own accuracy.
  • The spend ladder cracks saturated channels — at a real cost. Multiple bracketed test cells measure the response curve that a single test can only assume; it is the one method that right-sizes saturated channels, but it costs a quarter of calendar time, scarce geo inventory, and a real media tax. See the spend-ladder analysis.

Recommendations

  1. Default to a regularised Bayesian MMM and report uncertainty on every number. Point estimates without intervals are how teams talk themselves into spurious precision.
  2. Always control seasonality and known demand drivers. If you cannot, assume your media is over-credited.
  3. Measure your confound. If spend correlates strongly with demand and you have no experiment, treat the decomposition as a hypothesis, not an answer.
  4. Triangulate with randomized geo experiments. A geo lift test / spend ladder is the only confound-immune evidence. Feed it curve-aware, not as a naive scalar prior.
  5. Decide on MARGINAL ROI under uncertainty, not average ROI — and split channels into confident moves (act now) vs test-first (run an experiment before reallocating).
  6. Check robustness. Refit on data subsets / time folds; if the ranking of channels flips, your "answer" is noise. One leaderboard is never enough.

Run it on your data

The recommendations above are packaged. draftzone_mmm.run.run_mmm fits the findings-optimised configuration — regularised priors, seasonality control, uncertainty everywhere, a confound diagnostic, marginal ROI, and a confident-vs-test-first recommendation:

from draftzone_mmm.run import run_mmm

res = run_mmm(
    df,                                  # one row per week
    kpi="conversions",                   # your outcome column
    channels=["tv", "search", "social"],
    spend_suffix="_spend",               # -> tv_spend, search_spend, ...
    exposure_suffix="_impressions",      # optional; defaults to spend
    controls=["price", "promo"],         # optional observed confounders
    date="week",
)

print(res.summary())                     # contributions+CIs, confound, marginal ROI, verdicts
res.contributions    # per-channel avg contribution with 89% credible intervals
res.roi              # average AND marginal ROI per channel
res.recommend()      # scale-up / cut (confident) vs test-first (uncertain)

It is a single well-configured engine, deliberately conservative: when the confound is high and the posterior is wide it will tell you to test first rather than hand you a false answer. For a head-to-head of engines on your data, use the competition harness.
