Which engine when?

Does the best MMM depend on your data? A factorial sweep over channel saturation (3 regimes × 4 seeds), every fast engine graded per regime. ← multi-seed leaderboard · results · how it works

The multi-seed leaderboard says no engine is best on average. This page asks the sharper question: is a given engine best for a given kind of data? We hold everything fixed and vary only saturation — from heavily saturated channels (little headroom) to near-linear ones — and re-rank the engines in each regime.

Yes — the best engine depends on the data. Holding everything else fixed and varying only saturation, the rankings move enough to change the recommendation. The most regime-sensitive engine is Google Meridian (avg rank 1.8 when saturated → 6.8 with headroom).

The experiment anchor flips sign with saturation. Anchoring our Bayesian fit to the geo lift is worst when channels are saturated (rank 7.2) but good with headroom (rank 2.8): a lift test on a saturated channel is a small, noisy signal whose curve-aware translation is unreliable, whereas on a near-linear channel the lift is clean and the anchor helps. This refines the blunt 'the anchor hurts' rule into a conditional one.

The spend ladder is strongest with headroom, not saturation (best at low saturation, rank 2.5). With a near-linear response its curve fit is clean; heavy saturation gives it small, noisy lifts and it slips to the middle. (Its earlier billing — 'the ladder cracks saturated channels' — was about beating a single-cell experiment on those channels, not about beating observational engines on overall accuracy. The competition corrected the framing.)

The safe default is Meridian — lowest combined rank-and-spread, never bad in any regime. If you don't know your saturation, start there.

"Most reliable" = best average rank in that regime across the seed set. With a handful of seeds per regime this is directional, not a verdict — the honest output is a tendency, and the project's first rule still holds: where the confound is high, triangulate with an experiment rather than trusting any single observational engine.

What holds regardless of regime

  • Regularise — the unregularised engines are the ones that diverge, in every regime.
  • Control seasonality and measure your confound.
  • Experiments beat confounding — but read the conditional result honestly: a lift test is a clean signal on a near-linear channel and a small, noisy one on a saturated channel, so the experiment anchor helps with headroom and hurts when saturated.
  • Naive and frequentist regression never win a regime — there is no kind of data here where the un-regularised or un-saturated baselines are the right tool.
