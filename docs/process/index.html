A Skeptic's Guide to Marketing Mix Modeling

How the MMM competition works

Sealed-truth grading, a dozen engines, and a multi-seed protocol.

Why a competition

On real data you never learn whether your MMM was right — there is no answer key. So we built a synthetic world where there is one. Every channel's true carryover, saturation, and effect are generated from known parameters and sealed in data_sealed/ground_truth.json. A hard rule, enforced by a test, keeps the modeling pipeline from ever reading it; only the grader may. Every engine then competes to recover the truth it cannot see.

The synthetic world (the data-generating process)

  • Channels with distinct physics. Six media channels, each with its own carryover (geometric adstock θ from 0.10 to 0.75) and diminishing returns (Hill saturation), spanning saturated (paid_social, paid_search, tv) and headroom (influencer, dooh) channels.
  • The confound. Spend is paced to demand, so total spend correlates ~0.6 with seasonality — the central reason naive attribution is biased.
  • Controls + noise. Promotions, price, competitor pressure, holidays, plus realistic noise.
  • Experiments. A rotating per-channel geo lift test, and a multi-cell spend ladder that brackets each channel's operating point to measure (not assume) its response curve.
  • A hardened geo panel. A 50-geo world with a latent, unobserved demand confounder (affluent DMAs spend more and convert more) — to test whether geo-level models survive a real confound.

The competitors

  • Naive OLS — linear regression on spend. The blunt baseline.
  • Frequentist NLS — non-linear least squares with adstock+Hill (now ridge-regularised).
  • DraftZone PyMC — our regularised Bayesian MMM, observational and (optionally) experiment-anchored.
  • Google Meridian — the real package, with Fourier vs its native AKS seasonality, and on the multi-geo panel with a spectrum of demand-proxy controls.
  • Meta Robyn — the real R package (ridge + Nevergrad + Prophet + DECOMP.RSSD), plain and experiment-calibrated, plus a faithful Python reimplementation.
  • Spend ladder — a pure-experiment readout that fits the response curve from bracketed test cells.

How we score

  • MAE per channel — mean absolute error of recovered avg weekly contribution vs sealed truth. The headline metric.
  • Media bias — total recovered media vs truth (over/under-crediting).
  • Interval coverage — for Bayesian engines, does the 89% credible interval contain the truth?
  • Sampling quality (ESS / R-hat) — a recovery you couldn't sample isn't a recovery.
  • Divergence guard — an engine whose fit blew up is flagged and kept out of the chart.

Geo engines are graded against the geo world's own answer key (it differs from national by a small aggregation gap plus the confounder), never the national one.

The robustness protocol — why one dataset is never enough

A single graded dataset answers "who won this world?"; that ranking is unstable. So we regenerate the entire world under many seeds and aggregate by average rank, win-rate, and MAE spread. Each seed is independent — new data, new experiments, new sealed truth — so nothing is reused; every engine is recomputed every run. The honest output is a distribution with overlap, not a podium. Tellingly, even the robustness ranking shifted between 6 and 10 seeds: at 6 seeds one engine looked like a clear champion; at 10 its lead was inside the noise. Stability claims need samples too.
